# Lecture 2: Psychological Data

## Types of Study and Designs
Research can be either **experimental** (manipulating the IV) or **observational** (recording variables without manipulation, making causal inference harder). 
Study designs include:
- **Within-subjects designs**: All participants receive all levels of the IV.
- **Between-subjects designs**: Participants are separated into groups receiving one level of the IV.
- **Mixed designs**: Combining within and between-subject variables.
*(Source: Lecture 2. Psychological data - notes.pdf)*

## Types of Data and Measurement
Data comes in forms like character strings, binary (1 or 0), integers, or real numbers. 
Variables are categorized by **Levels of Measurement (N.O.I.R.)**:
- **Nominal**: Categories with no logical order (e.g., transport mode).
- **Ordinal**: Ordered categories where distance isn't meaningful (e.g., qualification).
- **Interval**: Meaningful distance but no absolute zero (e.g., IQ, temperature).
- **Ratio**: Meaningful zero point (e.g., reaction time).

**Likert scales** are technically ordinal but typically treated as interval in psychological practice. See [[Variables_and_Measurement]] for more.

In datasets, missing data points are usually represented as `NA`.

## Reliability and Validity
Two crucial concepts for evaluating measures are reliability and validity:
- **Reliability**: Consistency of measurement. Types include **test-retest**, **inter-rater**, and **internal consistency**.
- **Validity**: Whether the measure accurately assesses the underlying construct. Types include **face validity**, **convergent validity**, **discriminant validity**, and **predictive validity**.

Poor reliability introduces **measurement error**, making it difficult to observe real relationships. 
Threats to study validity include **confounding variables** (threatening internal validity) and **artefacts** (threatening external validity/generalisability).
