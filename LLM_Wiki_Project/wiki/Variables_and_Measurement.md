# Variables and Measurement

In psychological research, variables must be rigorously defined and measured. This forms a core component of the [[Research_Process]].

## Levels of Measurement (N.O.I.R.)
Variables differ in the amount of mathematical information they contain:
1. **Nominal**: Unordered categories (e.g., breakfast cereal). Only equality (=) can be compared.
2. **Ordinal**: Ordered categories without meaningful distances (e.g., rank in a race). Can compare magnitude (<, >).
3. **Interval**: Regular intervals but no absolute zero (e.g., IQ). Can perform addition/subtraction.
4. **Ratio**: Regular intervals with a meaningful zero (e.g., reaction time). All mathematical operations are valid.

*Note on Likert Scales*: Rating scales (e.g., 1-7 satisfaction) are technically ordinal, but psychologists commonly treat them as interval data.

## Study Designs
- **Experimental**: Involves manipulating the independent variable.
- **Observational**: Involves simply recording variables.
- **Within-subjects**: Participants experience all conditions.
- **Between-subjects**: Participants are split into different condition groups.

## Reliability and Validity
No measurement is perfect; researchers must minimize **measurement error**.
- **Reliability** is the consistency of a measure (Test-retest, Inter-rater, Internal consistency).
- **Validity** is the accuracy of a measure—does it measure the intended construct? 
  - *Construct validity* encompasses Convergent (correlates with similar tests), Discriminant (distinguishes from unrelated tests), and Predictive validity.
  - *Internal validity* refers to the integrity of the findings, threatened by **confounding variables**.
  - *External validity* refers to generalisability, threatened by **artefacts** of the specific experimental setting.
