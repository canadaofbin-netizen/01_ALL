# Kornysheva Lab (Skilled Action and Memory Lab)

## Background
The Skilled Action and Memory Lab, led by Professor Katja Kornysheva at the School of Psychology and the Centre for Human Brain Health (CHBH), [[University_of_Birmingham]], is a world-renowned research group that investigates how the human brain plans and controls the sequence and timing of complex, skilled actions, such as playing a musical instrument or typing. This lab re-evaluates conventional neuroscientific wisdom and is laying the theoretical and technological foundations for applying next-generation non-invasive [[Brain_Computer_Interface]] technologies to motor disorder rehabilitation.

## Key Attributes

### Competitive Queuing Model
The core theoretical foundation is the [[Competitive_Queuing]] (CQ) model, which posits that before a human executes a sequence of movements, individual action elements are prepared in parallel within the brain, forming a gradient of activation strength according to their order.

### Landmark Research
- **Journal of Neuroscience (2024)**: First in the world to prove that the hippocampus — traditionally thought to be limited to episodic memory — is directly involved in pre-planning procedural motor skill sequences. Study used multivariate pattern analysis (MVPA) of fMRI on 24 participants with a custom force-transducer-equipped keyboard (first author: Rhys Yewbrey). Distinguished the high-level sequence control role of the anterior hippocampus from the lower-level control of cortico-striato-cerebellar circuits.
- **DCD/Dyspraxia Study (2026)**: Identified that motor planning deficits in Developmental Coordination Disorder patients stem from a significant degradation in their inherent ability to pre-order movement sequences (CQ gradient formation), not from general working memory problems. Also confirmed that even in kinematically fused actions like continuous handwriting, individual movement elements are still competitively pre-ordered.

### Major Funding
- **UKRI Future Leaders Fellowship**: £2.1 million to develop neurotechnological tools for stroke and Parkinson's disease patients. Collaboration with EPFL and Imperial College London.
- **ARIA Opportunity Seed Funding** (September 2025): [[Scalable_Neural_Interfaces]] research in collaboration with Professors Oiwi Parker Jones and Ole Jensen (Oxford University) and Dr. Diar Abdlkarim (Birmingham). Core architecture: Constructing massive multi-modal foundation datasets combining high-density [[Electroencephalography]] and MEG with kinematic data, then fed into GPT-based foundation models to decode intricate hand and arm movement intentions.

### Equipment & Tech Stack
- **Equipment**: MRI, conventional MEG, OPM-MEG, Focused Ultrasound Stimulation (FUS), TMS, fNIRS, high-density EEG.
- **Core Skills**: MEG/EEG/fMRI data acquisition and preprocessing, MVPA, LDA, Python and MATLAB proficiency, GPT-based model design, force transducer sensor control, kinematics analysis.
- **Expansion**: Announced aggressive recruitment of 3 postdocs and 2 RAs.
