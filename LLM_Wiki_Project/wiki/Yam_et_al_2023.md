# Summary: The Rise of Robots Increases Job Insecurity and Maladaptive Workplace Behaviors

**Authors**: Kai Chi Yam, Pok Man Tang, Joshua Conrad Jackson, Runkun Su, and Kurt Gray (2023)

## Core Empirical Findings
Through a series of studies, the authors consistently found that exposure to robots—whether physical or psychological—increases employee [[Job_Insecurity]]:
- **Study 1 (Archival, USA)**: Higher industrial robot density predicted higher rates of job-recruiting site searches, even though robot density was not associated with actual unemployment rates.
- **Study 3 (Experience-Sampling, India)**: Engineers working daily with robots experienced increased [[Job_Insecurity]] as daily robot exposure increased, which mediated increases in burnout and [[Counterproductive_Work_Behavior]] (incivility).
- **Study 4 (Experiment, USA)**: A self-affirmation intervention successfully buffered the negative effects of robot exposure on [[Job_Insecurity]].

## Theoretical Contributions
- **Cognitive Appraisal Theory of Stress**: Applies this theory to human-robot interaction within the [[AI_and_Work]] domain, illustrating that exposure to robots acts as a threatening environmental stimulus.
- **Subjective vs. Objective Threat**: Provides evidence that the psychological threat of robots occurs independently of actual job loss.
