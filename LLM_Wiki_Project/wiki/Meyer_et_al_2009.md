# A meta-analytic investigation into the moderating effects of situational strength on the conscientiousness–performance relationship

**Authors**: Rustin D. Meyer, Reeshad S. Dalal, and Silvia Bonaccio (2009)

## Core Empirical Findings
This [[Meta_Analysis]] demonstrates that occupation-level [[Situational_Strength]] significantly moderates the relationship between conscientiousness and job performance. Specifically, the criterion-related validity of conscientiousness is higher in "weak" occupations (where behavioral cues and restrictions are low) than in "strong" occupations. 

## Theoretical Contributions
The study contributes to interactionist theory by conceptualizing and operationalizing situational strength at the occupational level. It distills situational strength into two primary, theoretically meaningful facets: 
1. **Constraints**: The extent to which an employee's behavioral/decisional discretion is restricted by external forces (e.g., policies, strict procedures).
2. **Consequences**: The presence of contingencies between an employee's actions and important outcomes (e.g., risks to health, safety, or financial resources).

## Methodology
- **Data**: Meta-analysis of 114 primary studies (N = 34,659 individuals).
- **Situational Strength Operationalization**: Used the Occupational Information Network (O*NET) database to quantify constraints and consequences.
- **Analysis**: Modified Weighted Least Squares (WLS) regression.
