# The Person–Situation Debate Revisited (Judge & Zapata, 2015)

## Theoretical Contributions
This paper theoretically integrates two distinct interactionist perspectives—[[Situational_Strength]] (general interactionism) and [[Trait_Activation_Theory]] (specific interactionism)—into a unified conceptual framework. The authors demonstrate how occupational contexts moderate the relationship between the Big Five personality traits and job performance.

## Methodology
Data from 125 previously published studies were analyzed using the O*NET database to objectively code the job contexts of the homogeneous occupations studied.

## Core Empirical Findings
- **Situation Strength (Process):** All Big Five traits were more predictive of job performance in "weak" situations—specifically, job processes characterized by unstructured work, freedom to make decisions, and variety.
- **Trait Activation:** Traits predicted performance better in jobs that provided trait-relevant cues (e.g. Conscientiousness validity was stronger in occupations requiring independence).
- **Relative Importance:** Dominance analyses revealed that [[Trait_Activation_Theory]] was generally more important than [[Situational_Strength]] in explaining the variance in personality–performance validities for four of the five traits.
