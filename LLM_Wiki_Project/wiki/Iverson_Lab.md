# Iverson Lab

## Background
Professor Paul Iverson's research group at the Department of Speech, Hearing & Phonetic Sciences at [[University_College_London]] possesses a unique research philosophy. Rather than developing classical motor-controlled BCIs, they deeply analyze — via [[Electroencephalography]] and machine learning — how the human brain adapts auditory and lexical processing when a listener in a multilingual environment learns and perceives their native language, foreign languages, or various accents in daily life.

## Key Attributes

### Hardware Innovation: Dry-Electrode Wearable EEG System
The lab is developing a proprietary wearable, head-mounted EEG recorder based on dry-electrodes combined with a microphone-array, supported by a massive grant from the UK Economic and Social Research Council (ESRC, Project No: ES/Z503630/1). Key features:
- **Microphone-array AI filtering**: An ultra-compact multiple microphone-array integrated into a wearable form factor clearly separates only a specific speaker's voice from environmental noise.
- **Gel-free dry EEG electrodes**: Real-time synchronization of auditory and lexical neural processing signals from 9-month-old infants in multilingual households or adult learners who have migrated to the UK.
- **Open-source**: Hardware blueprints and signal processing code will be open-sourced to the scientific community.

### LLM-Based Neural Tracking (eNeuro, 2024)
In a pioneering study published in eNeuro in August 2024 ("Neural Tracking of Speech Acoustics in Noise Is Coupled with Lexical Predictability as Estimated by Large Language Models"), the team:
- Used GPT-4 to probabilistically quantify the "Lexical Predictability" of each spoken word.
- Combined this with EEG delta and theta band oscillation analysis.
- Discovered that when "lexically harder" words (low probability based on context) are presented, the listener's acoustic tracking signals amplify more strongly, suggesting that listeners unconsciously increase cognitive effort (listening effort) to capture acoustic features.
- This strongly suggests that speech perception is a highly dynamic process where top-down cognitive prediction intervenes in the acoustic processing stage in real-time — not merely a feedforward sensory pipeline.

### Auditory ERP Findings
Previous research also found that N400 lexical processing EEG responses (see [[Event_Related_Potentials]]) and neural entrainment to the target speaker increase when listeners are exposed to non-native (L2) English or unfamiliar accents.

### Networking & Recruitment
- In February 2025, recruited a researcher dedicated to hardware development, AI-based input signal inspection, and open-sourcing code.
- Ideal for researchers interested in dry EEG electrode hardware, AI acoustic filtering, and building real-time data collection pipelines.
- Strategic networking target for BCI/EEG researchers living near London.
