# Lecture 15: Reproducible Science

## The Reproducibility Crisis
Concerns that many published effects (e.g., social priming, power poses) do not replicate. Large-scale replication efforts found less than half of major studies replicated successfully.
*(Source: Lecture 15. Reproducible science.pdf)*

## Threats to Reproducibility
- **Fraud and Sloppiness**: Data manipulation.
- **Publication Bias**: The "file drawer problem" where null results are not published.
- **Low Power**: Small sample sizes combined with small true effects yield low positive predictive value.
- **HARKing**: Hypothesizing after results are known.
- **p-hacking**: Manipulating data analysis to achieve significance (p < .05).

## Open Science Solutions
- **Preregistration**: Submitting hypotheses and analysis plans before data collection.
- **Open Data**: Sharing data for reproduction and secondary analysis.
- **Reproducible Analysis**: Using scripts (like R) to document analytical decisions.
