# Lecture 1: The Research Process

## Introduction
This lecture outlines the introductory statistical methods module, emphasizing the shift from SPSS to R for data analysis. R allows for reproducible reports and is a valuable, transferable coding skill. The lecture also emphasizes that maths and coding skills are determined by practice, addressing common statistics anxiety.
*(Source: Lecture 1. The Research Process - notes.pdf)*

## Empirical Research
Modern science is based on **empirical research** rather than intuition. This research falls into two broad categories:
- **Qualitative research**: Involves data that cannot be quantified (e.g., interviews, focus groups).
- **Quantitative research**: Involves quantifiable data (e.g., questionnaire scores, reaction times).
*(Source: Lecture 1. The Research Process - notes.pdf)*

## Steps of the Research Process
The core of the lecture is the 8-step [[Research_Process]]:
1. **Choose a research question** (e.g., Does sleep impact memory?)
2. **Select dependent and independent variables**: 
   - Independent variable (IV, predictor) is doing the explaining.
   - Dependent variable (DV, outcome) is being explained.
3. **Operationalise the variables**: Transforming theoretical constructs into concrete measurements (e.g., measuring "memory" via a functioning questionnaire). This relates closely to [[Variables_and_Measurement]].
4. **State your hypothesis**: A testable belief based on theory.
5. **Select a sample from the population**: The sample must be representative of the target population.
6. **Collect data**: Put into a usable format, handle missing data, and score items.
7. **Analyse data**: Using [[Descriptive_Statistics]] and inferential statistical tests.
8. **Draw conclusions**: Determine if data supports the hypothesis, acknowledging that no single study is conclusive. Revise theories accordingly.
