# Descriptive Statistics

In psychological research (see [[Research_Process]]), descriptive statistics are crucial for summarising and making sense of large datasets, facilitating generalisation and prediction.

## Measures of Central Tendency
Central tendency represents the "average" or typical value of a distribution.
- **Mode**: The most frequent value; primarily used for nominal data.
- **Mean**: The mathematical average. It is sensitive to extreme outliers and skewed distributions.
- **Median**: The middle value of an ordered dataset. Often a better representation of central tendency when the data is heavily skewed.

## Measures of Dispersion
Dispersion measures how far, on average, data points are spread from the mean.
1. **Sum of Squared Differences**: The sum of squared distances of each point from the mean.
2. **Variance**: The sum of squared differences divided by `N - 1` (degrees of freedom for a sample).
3. **Standard Deviation (SD)**: The square root of the variance, converting the metric back into the original units of measurement.
4. **Covariance**: A measure of how two variables vary together. (See [[Correlation]] for more on covariance and Pearson's r.)

## Z-Scores
**Z-scores** standardise scores, representing them as the number of standard deviations a specific data point is from the sample mean. In a normal distribution, approximately 68.2% of data points fall within 1 SD of the mean, and 95.4% fall within 2 SDs.
