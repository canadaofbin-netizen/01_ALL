# Sampling

In psychological research, sampling is the process of selecting a subset of participants from a broader population in order to infer findings about that population.

## Types of Sampling
- **Random Sampling**: Every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected. This reduces bias and makes the sample more representative, though it is often practically difficult.
- **Convenience Sampling**: Recruiting individuals who are easily accessible (e.g., undergraduate psychology students).

## Sampling Error and Bias
- **Sampling Error**: The difference between the sample mean and the true population mean. It can be reduced by increasing the sample size.
- **Sampling Bias**: Occurs when the non-randomness of the sample affects the outcome. If the selection bias is unrelated to the outcome of interest, it may not pose a problem.

## Sampling Distribution
The **sampling distribution** of the mean represents the distribution of means of all possible samples of a certain size from a population.
- The mean of the sample means is equal to the population mean.
- According to the **Central Limit Theorem**, as the sample size increases, the sampling distribution of the mean approaches a normal distribution, regardless of the shape of the population distribution.

## Standard Error
The variation in the sampling distribution is called the **Standard Error (SE)**. It is estimated by dividing the sample's standard deviation by the square root of the sample size ($SE = SD / \sqrt{N}$). This measure is essential for calculating confidence intervals and determining statistical significance in tests such as the [[T_Test]].
