# **Kim Lab Journal Club**

## **Reading List & Presentation Guide**

**Format:**

* Two article summaries per week (5–10 minutes each)  
* Assigned readings are empirical articles. Review and conceptual pieces (and more empirical pieces in some weeks) are listed as recommended readings for each week.

**Themes:**

* Situational Strength & Related Topics (Weeks 1–2)  
* Artificial Intelligence & Work (Weeks 3–6)

## **Overview Structure Guide (10-Minute Format)**

*Use this template as guidelines to prepare your article overview. Follow this structure so every presentation is comparable and discussion can build across weeks. You’ll see a few (or more) questions under each section. These are some (not all) of the questions that you want to ask when reading an academic journal article. Note, however, that you do **not** have to answer all those questions in your article overview; some of them are just reiterations of another.*

1. **Title & Motivation (\~2 minutes)**   
   Related questions include but are not limited to:   
   1. What is the paper about? State the topic in one sentence.  
   2. Why does this research matter? What gap in the literature does it address?  
   3. What was unknown or contested before this study?  
   4. What is the core research question?

   **Tip**: Think about this as answering “Why should we care?” before you describe what they did.

2. **The “So What” / Implications (\~1.5 minutes)**  
   1. What are the theoretical contributions? Does it introduce, extend, or test a theory?  
   2. What are the practical implications for organizations or managers?  
   3. Why is this finding important beyond the paper itself?

   **Tip**: Think of this as answering the question: “If this is true, what does it change?”

3. **Research Design & Method (\~2 minutes)**  
   1. What was the study design? (e.g., cross-sectional survey, longitudinal, ESM, experiment, meta-analysis)  
   2. Who were the participants / what was the sample?  
   3. How were the key constructs measured?  
   4. List the key predictor variables, mediator variables (if any), moderator variables (if any), and outcome/dependent variables.

   **Tip**: You do not need to explain every analysis—just the design logic and the variable structure.

4. **Key Results (\~2.5 minutes)**  
   1. What were the main findings? Focus on the most important results.  
   2. Were hypotheses supported or not? Any surprising null findings?  
   3. If there were moderation or mediation findings, briefly describe the pattern.

   **Tip**: Use plain language. You do not need to report every statistic — pick the 2–3 findings that matter most.

5. **Takeaways & Discussion Prompt (\~2 minutes)**  
   1. What are the 2–3 most important things to remember from this paper?  
   2. What are the key limitations of the study?  
   3. Pose one discussion question for the group.

   **Tip**: Your discussion question should connect to something we've read before, or something you found surprising or debatable.

**General Tips:**

* Practice once out loud before the meeting. Ten minutes goes faster than you think.  
* Prepare for scripts but don’t read from them; be able to talk about them.  
* Prioritize clarity over comprehensiveness. It is better to explain two things well than to rush through ten.  
* It is okay to say “I'm not sure.” The point of these meetings is to learn together.
